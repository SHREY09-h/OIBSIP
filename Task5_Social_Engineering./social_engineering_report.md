# Social Engineering Attacks: Types, Impact, and Prevention

## Introduction

Social engineering is a cyberattack technique that manipulates people into revealing sensitive information or performing actions that compromise security. Instead of exploiting technical vulnerabilities, attackers exploit human behavior and trust.

## Types of Social Engineering Attacks

### 1. Phishing

Phishing is one of the most common social engineering attacks. Attackers send fraudulent emails, messages, or websites that appear legitimate to trick users into providing passwords, banking information, or other sensitive data.

#### Example

An attacker sends an email pretending to be a bank and asks users to verify their account details through a fake website.

### 2. Pretexting

Pretexting occurs when an attacker creates a fabricated scenario to gain the victim's trust and obtain confidential information.

#### Example

An attacker pretends to be an IT support employee and asks an employee for login credentials.

### 3. Baiting

Baiting involves offering something attractive to lure victims into compromising their security.

#### Example

An attacker leaves an infected USB drive in a public place. When someone plugs it into their computer, malware is installed.

### 4. Tailgating

Tailgating occurs when an unauthorized individual gains physical access to a restricted area by following an authorized person.

#### Example

An attacker follows an employee through a secure entrance without proper authorization.

## Real-World Case Studies

### Twitter Bitcoin Scam (2020)

Attackers used social engineering techniques to gain access to internal Twitter systems and compromise high-profile accounts. The incident resulted in financial losses and reputational damage.

### Target Data Breach (2013)

Attackers gained access to Target's network through a third-party vendor using stolen credentials. The breach exposed millions of customer records.

## Impact on Organizations

* Financial losses
* Data breaches
* Identity theft
* Reputation damage
* Operational disruption
* Legal and regulatory consequences

## Prevention Measures

### Employee Awareness Training

Regular cybersecurity awareness programs help employees recognize social engineering attempts.

### Multi-Factor Authentication (MFA)

MFA adds an additional layer of security even if passwords are compromised.

### Verification Procedures

Employees should verify requests for sensitive information through official channels.

### Strong Security Policies

Organizations should establish clear security policies and incident reporting procedures.

### Regular Security Assessments

Security audits and penetration testing help identify weaknesses before attackers exploit them.

## Conclusion

Social engineering attacks target human behavior rather than technical vulnerabilities. Organizations can reduce the risk of these attacks through employee training, strong authentication methods, and effective security policies.
